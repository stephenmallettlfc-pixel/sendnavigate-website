What the Ofsted Inclusion Judgement Means for Your School — and How to Prepare

From November 2025, inclusion is a standalone judgement on every school's Ofsted report card. Here's what inspectors look for — and how to ensure your staff are ready.

Every school in England is now inspected against a new standalone inclusion judgement. For the first time, inclusion is not buried inside a broader quality of education grade — it has its own rating, its own evidence base, and its own entry on your report card.

If your school is inspected under the new framework, inspectors will make a specific assessment of how well you identify and support pupils who face barriers to learning. They will look at outcomes for disadvantaged pupils, pupils with SEND, and pupils known to social care — separately and specifically.

This is new territory for most schools. And it lands at exactly the moment when SEND identification is rising, NHS waiting lists for autism and ADHD assessments exceed two years, and almost 25% of mainstream staff report receiving no neurodiversity training at all.

What Inspectors Will Look For

The new framework asks inspectors to evaluate whether:

  • Staff can identify barriers to learning early — before they become behaviour incidents or exclusions
  • Reasonable adjustments are being made consistently across the school, not just in SEND-designated provision
  • Teachers in mainstream classrooms — not only SENCOs and TAs — understand how to adapt their teaching for pupils with unmet needs
  • The school has evidence that staff have received meaningful training on SEND and inclusion, not just completed an e-learning module

The five-point scale means there is no longer a pass/fail. There is a spectrum, and your position on that spectrum will depend on what inspectors observe in classrooms and what evidence you can produce.

The Evidence Problem

For most schools, the challenge is not that inclusion is absent — it is that inclusion is unevenly distributed. A few highly skilled SENCOs and experienced TAs carry provision that the broader staff body cannot replicate when they are absent, overwhelmed, or dealing with a different pupil.

Inspectors will look at what happens in period three on a Tuesday in a mainstream English or maths lesson when the SENCO is in a meeting. That is the test. Not the support plan. Not the EHCP. What the classroom teacher does, in real time, with a pupil who is struggling.

That is the gap the Ofsted inclusion judgement exposes. And it is the gap that most existing SEND training does not close — because most training teaches staff about SEND, not what to do when a pupil is struggling in front of them.

What Strong Preparation Looks Like

Schools that are well-placed under the new framework share three characteristics:

1. Whole-staff training, not SENCO-only training. Inspectors want to see that every teacher — not just designated support staff — has received training on identifying and responding to SEND in their classroom.

2. Practical, classroom-level skills. Training that teaches what SEND is does not prepare staff for what to do when a pupil is struggling in front of them. The most defensible evidence is staff who can demonstrate a consistent, teachable method for in-the-moment identification and response.

3. A documented training record. A formal training register — showing who completed what, when, and to what standard — is the evidence base you need. CPD-accredited training produces official certificates. An in-house session without accreditation is harder to evidence to an inspector.
