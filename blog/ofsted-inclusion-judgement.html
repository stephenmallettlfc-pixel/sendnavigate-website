What the Ofsted Inclusion Judgement Actually Means for Classroom Teachers

Ofsted & Inspection July 2026 Stephen Mallett, Former Headteacher 8 min read

In November 2025, Ofsted quietly changed something that will affect every teacher in every mainstream school in England.

Inclusion is now a standalone judgement.

It is no longer folded inside "quality of education" or referenced briefly in the leadership section. Inspectors award a separate grade for inclusion — on a five-point scale from Exemplary to Causing Concern — and that grade appears on your school's report card alongside everything else.

Here is what that means in practice, and why most schools are less ready for it than they think.

The Old Model vs the New One

Under the previous framework, inclusion was largely assessed by looking at outcomes data — exclusion rates, attendance gaps, attainment for disadvantaged pupils. Inspectors inferred quality of inclusion from the numbers.

The new framework is more direct. Inspectors want to see what happens in classrooms. They want to observe teachers identifying and responding to SEND in real time — not in SENCO-run sessions, not in dedicated TA support, but in mainstream lessons, delivered by mainstream teachers, every day.

This shifts the accountability from a small team of specialists to every adult in the building.

What Inspectors Will Actually Watch

When an inspector sits in a Year 8 maths lesson with a pupil with undiagnosed ADHD and a pupil who is on the autism spectrum, they will be watching whether the teacher:

  • Notices that one pupil has disengaged after the second instruction
  • Adjusts pacing or delivery without singling the pupil out
  • Uses the kind of low-effort, high-impact strategies that research consistently shows work — chunking, visual scaffolding, predictable routines — without making a performance of it

They will also ask: has this teacher been trained to do this? Can they articulate why they made the adjustments they made?

Most teachers cannot — not because they do not care, but because most SEND training does not teach them to. It teaches them about autism. About ADHD. About dyslexia. It does not teach them what to do with a pupil in front of them in period three on a Tuesday.

The Gap That Needs Closing

There is a reason over 60% of teachers report feeling unprepared to support SEND after their initial training. Initial teacher training covers SEND as a category — a topic in a module, not a practice embedded in pedagogy.

Continuing professional development has largely followed the same model. E-learning modules explaining what autism is. INSET sessions on attachment theory. Awareness-raising content that leaves staff informed about SEND but no more skilled at identifying and responding to it in their classroom.

The Ofsted inclusion judgement asks schools to move from awareness to competency. That is a different ask — and it requires different training.

What Strong Evidence Looks Like

For a school to perform well under the new framework, inspectors need to see:

Whole-staff, documented training. Not SENCO-only CPD. Not a single external trainer who came in once. A structured programme that every member of teaching and support staff has completed, with a training register that proves it.

Training that is CPD-accredited. Informal in-house sessions are hard to evidence. Programmes that produce official CPD certificates give inspectors something tangible — and give staff something to record in their own professional portfolios.

Practical skills, not just knowledge. The training evidence that impresses inspectors is not "we did an INSET on autism." It is "every teacher in this school has completed a programme that gives them a method for identifying and responding to SEND in their lesson, and we can show you the certificates."

What This Means If Your Inspection Is Coming

If your school has not had SEND training for your whole staff in the past 12 months — or if the training you have done is awareness-focused rather than skills-focused — the inclusion judgement is a risk.

The practical response is not to panic but to move quickly. A structured, INSET-ready programme, delivered internally by your own staff, with CPD accreditation for every participant, is achievable in a single term. You do not need an external trainer or a large budget. You need content that is ready to deliver, staff who can facilitate it, and a system that records completion.

The school that is ready for the Ofsted inclusion judgement is not the school with the most sophisticated EHCP paperwork. It is the school where every teacher knows what to look for, and what to do next.
