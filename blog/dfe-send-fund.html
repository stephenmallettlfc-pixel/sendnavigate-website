How Schools Can Use the DfE £200m SEND Fund for Whole-Staff Training

Funding & Policy July 2026 Stephen Mallett, Former Headteacher 7 min read

In January 2026, the Department for Education announced a £200 million investment in SEND teacher training — the largest commitment to SEND workforce development in a generation.

Most headteachers have heard of it. Far fewer know how to access it, what it funds, or what kinds of training actually qualify.

Here is a plain-English guide.

What the £200m Fund Is For

The programme is designed to do three things:

  1. Provide whole-staff SEND training to all teaching and support staff — not just SENCOs and designated staff
  2. Fund the development and distribution of high-quality training materials to every school and college in England
  3. Build capacity in mainstream schools so that pupils with SEND are supported before they reach crisis — reducing pressure on the EHCP system, specialist provision, and exclusion

The explicit aim is to shift SEND support into the mainstream classroom. The DfE's own framing is clear: this money is intended to change what happens in everyday lessons, not to expand specialist resource bases.

Who Can Access It

The fund is available to:

  • Individual maintained schools and academies — for whole-staff SEND CPD programmes
  • Multi-Academy Trusts — for trust-wide training that covers all schools under a single licence
  • Local Authorities — for programmes that support mainstream schools across the LA area

Schools do not need to apply through a competitive tender for all spend. Much of the budget flows through Teaching School Hubs and LA-level allocations. Talk to your Teaching School Hub to understand the local procurement route and timescales in your area.

What Qualifies as Eligible Spend

The fund is explicitly for CPD that:

  • Covers SEND identification and support for mainstream classroom teachers (not only specialist staff)
  • Is delivered to all staff, not selected individuals
  • Is evidence-informed — ideally aligned to frameworks like the EEF's guidance on SEND
  • Produces a documented record of completion

CPD-accredited programmes are the most straightforward to justify — they come with official certificates and a completion register that you can produce to your Teaching School Hub, LA, or Ofsted inspector.

Key point: Training that simply raises awareness does not fully meet the fund's intent. The DfE's emphasis on "practical strategies for supporting children with SEND" means skills-based training — what to do in the classroom — is exactly what the fund is designed to purchase.

The Procurement Window Is Now

Local authorities and teaching school hubs are in active procurement for the 2026–27 academic year. Schools that have not yet identified and budgeted their SEND CPD spend are behind the curve.

The practical reality is that school budgets are set annually. If SEND training is not in next year's professional development budget — specifically earmarked as fund-eligible spend — the money may not be available when you want it.

If you are a SBM or finance lead reading this: the question to resolve before the end of term is whether your school has identified an eligible, whole-staff SEND CPD programme to fund from this allocation.

What to Look For in an Eligible Programme

Based on the DfE's stated intent, a strong eligible programme will:

  • Be INSET-ready — no additional external trainer cost, which stretches the fund further
  • Cover all staff, not just SENCOs and TAs
  • Be CPD-accredited, so every participant receives a verifiable certificate
  • Be grounded in evidence-based frameworks — EEF Five-a-Day, Universal Design for Learning, or equivalent
  • Provide a completion record — a training register or dashboard that evidences participation for Ofsted
